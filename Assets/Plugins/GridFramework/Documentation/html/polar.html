Polar grids

The PolarGrid class

Polar grids are a subtype of LayeredGrid, two-dimensional polar grids are stacked as layers to form a cylindric grid.

The defining characteristics of polar grids are Radius, Sectors and Depth (inherited from LayeredGrid). There are two coordinate systems, a standard cylindrical one and a grid coordinate system similar to what the other grids use.

The Radius is the radius of the innermost circle around the origin and the radii of all the other circles are multiples of it. The next value, Sectors, tells us into how many sectors we divide the circles. It’s an integer value of at least one (the circle itself is one huge sector). We derive the angle between sectors by dividing 2π (or 360°) by the amount of sectors. We cannot set the angle directly, because the sum of all angles has to form a full circle, thus only certain values are possible. Finally, Depth gives us how far apart two grid planes are. If you want to use pure polar coordinates you can ignore this value, but it is relevant if you want to use cylindrical coordinates (i.e. 3D).

Note
Unless otherwise noted, all angles are given in radians.

Introduction to polar coordinates

Polar grids are based on polar coordinates. In the usual Cartesian coordinate system we identify a given point through its distance from the origin by looking at its distance from the coordinate axes (or if you prefer linear algebra, as the linear combination of the coordinate system’s basis vectors).

In a polar coordinate system we identify a point by its absolute distance (radius or radial coordinate) from the origin (pole) and its angle (angular coordinate, polar angle or azimuth) around a given axis (polar axis).

Cylindrical coordinates are polar coordinates with a third axis added for the distance (height or altitude) from the reference plane (the plane containing the origin). We call this third axis the cylindrical- or longitudinal axis.

Coordinate systems of polar grids

There are two coordinate systems supported: Grid and Cylindric; all angles are given in radians.

Cylindric coordinate system

A point in this coordinate system a point is given as a Vector3 where the first coordinate is the distance from the polar axis, the second one the angular component and the third one the distance from the central layer. All measurements are given in absolute units, so they are independent of the grid's Radius, Sectors and Depth.

The angle wraps around 2п, the angular value will never exceed this limit. If a larger value is passed to a method expecting cylindric coordinates it will wrap around and if the number is negative it will wrap around in the opposite direction.

This coordinate system is well-suited if you want to think in terms of “grid points”, for example in a board game. If you want to move the player one unit towards the outside and two units counter-clockwise you could write

var myPlayerPosition = myGrid.WorldToGrid(player.transform.position);
myPlayerPosition += new Vector3(1, 2, 0);
player.transform.position = myGrid.GridToWorld(myPlayerPosition);

Note how you don't need to know anything about the grid and its values, you exclusively concentrate on the movement itself.

Grid coordinate system

Similar to the Cylindric coordinate system, except the values do depend on the grid's Radius, Sectors and Depth. The first component is the distance from the polar axis as a multiple of Radius, the second one is the index of the sector the point is in and the third one is the distance from the central layer.

Renderers for polar grids

There is one official renderer for polar grids: the Cylinder. It draws a (partial) cylinder around the polar axis with a given starting and end radius, sector and layer.

The renderer has a Smoothness property that smooths out the arcs of the grid. In an ideal polar grid all arcs would be perfectly round, but Unity can't draw rounded shapes, instead we have to break a straight line down into smaller segments. The number of segments is the Smoothness and the higher the number the smoother the grid looks. A higher Smoothness also increases the number of points to compute and lines to render, impacting performance; you will have to find a good balance between look and speed. The more Sectors a grid has, the less Smoothness it needs.

Official extension methods

Polar grids support a number of useful conversions as extension methods. These extension methods convert angles to sectors and vice-versa, and they also support the generation of rotation quaternions based on angles and sectors.

There are also extension methods for finding the nearest vertex, face or cell given a point. Keep in mind that if the result is at the origin the value of the angular coordinate is undefined.
